Mumbai police arrested a McDonald's employee in 2012 after a hidden camera was discovered in the women's restroom of a franchise outlet, triggering public outrage and raising questions about surveillance and privacy in commercial establishments that serve millions of customers daily.

The camera, a small device concealed in the restroom, had apparently been recording for an undetermined period before a customer discovered it. The employee was arrested under provisions of the Information Technology Act as well as relevant sections of the Indian Penal Code dealing with voyeurism.

McDonald's India issued a statement condemning the incident, suspending operations at the outlet pending investigation, and pledging to cooperate fully with police. The company said it was reviewing security protocols at its locations nationwide.

The incident exposed a vulnerability that applies to any public facility with restrooms and limited oversight: the cost and miniaturization of hidden cameras has outpaced the informal social enforcement that previously made such installations impractical. Devices costing a few hundred rupees can be concealed in ordinary objects and stream footage remotely.

Privacy advocates called for mandatory periodic inspections of public restrooms in commercial establishments by police or regulatory authorities—a proposal that faces obvious practical challenges but addresses a genuine gap in current oversight frameworks.

For women using public facilities in India, the incident reinforced an already elevated level of vigilance. The legal response—arrest and prosecution—sends the right signal. The practical challenge is that detection depends primarily on customers rather than on any systematic oversight.

McDonald's India implemented additional training and inspection protocols in the aftermath.
