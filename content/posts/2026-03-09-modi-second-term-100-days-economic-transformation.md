Modi's third term, which began in 2024, entered 2026 with India's economy growing at 7%—respectable but below the 8-9% rates of pre-pandemic years. The first hundred days of his cabinet revealed a government consolidating rather than revolutionizing: continuing PLI schemes for manufacturing, pushing infrastructure, managing fiscal deficits.

Job creation remains the core challenge. Official statistics claim 2+ million formal jobs annually; reality appears more modest. Ten million enter the workforce yearly; formal sector creates 2-3 million jobs. The remainder enters informal economy. This mismatch constrains consumption and social stability.

Make in India's focus shifted to semiconductors and electronics, with Samsung, Intel promising capacity. Yet these won't produce significant employment until 2027-28. Manufacturing employment remains stuck around 12-13% of total—unchanged for a decade.

The fundamental problem is structural: India's growth depends on services (IT, finance) serving educated urban populations, not manufacturing absorbing rural workers. This creates a two-speed economy and limits how transformation can proceed.

Politically, the government faces pressure to spend ahead of 2027 elections. If growth slows, expansionary spending will intensify, challenging deficit consolidation targets. Expect continuity in approach rather than radical reform.
