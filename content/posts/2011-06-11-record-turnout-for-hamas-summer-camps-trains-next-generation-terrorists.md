Hamas is reporting record attendance at its summer camps for children in Gaza this year — a development that has drawn sharp criticism from Israeli officials, Western governments, and human rights observers who argue that the camps function as ideological and paramilitary training programs rather than ordinary youth activities.

The camps, which have operated annually for years, typically combine physical training, religious instruction, and what Hamas describes as resistance education. Critics point to photographs and video from previous years showing children in military-style uniforms handling weapons and undergoing drills. Hamas describes the activities as preparation for self-defense and characterizes criticism of the camps as part of a broader effort to delegitimize Palestinian resistance.

The record turnout this summer reflects both population growth in Gaza and what Hamas presents as increased community engagement with its programs. International aid organizations working in Gaza have consistently raised concerns about the militarization of children's experiences in the territory, pointing to the long-term psychological and social costs of raising a generation in an environment that normalizes armed conflict.

The broader context is a society under severe constraint. Gaza's population, blockaded for years, has limited access to the kind of civilian institutions — sports leagues, arts programs, university preparation — that might otherwise occupy adolescent energy and aspiration. In that vacuum, ideologically motivated organizations have been effective at filling the space.

Whether this year's camps represent a genuine expansion of Hamas's youth outreach or are primarily a reflection of demographic and circumstantial factors, the pattern is one that observers of the region have tracked with increasing concern for more than a decade.
