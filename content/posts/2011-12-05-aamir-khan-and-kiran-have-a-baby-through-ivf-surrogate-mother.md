Aamir Khan and his wife Kiran Rao announced the birth of their son Azad, who was born via IVF using a surrogate mother. The disclosure was handled with characteristic thoughtfulness by Khan, who acknowledged the process openly and expressed his gratitude to the surrogate.

The announcement brought surrogacy — a practice that had been growing in India but remained largely undiscussed in mainstream public culture — into open conversation in a way that a celebrity disclosure uniquely can. India had become one of the world's leading destinations for surrogacy, particularly for international couples, but the medical, ethical, and legal dimensions of the practice had received limited mainstream attention.

Kiran Rao explained that she had faced health complications during pregnancy, and that surrogacy had been the path that allowed them to have a child together. Aamir Khan emphasized that Azad's surrogate had been treated with full dignity and respect.

The announcement prompted a wider conversation about the experience of surrogates in India — women who carry children for other families under contractual arrangements that the law, at the time of Azad's birth, regulated lightly. Advocacy groups raised questions about whether women in economically vulnerable situations were making genuinely free choices, or whether financial necessity was functioning as a form of coercion.

India has since substantially tightened its surrogacy laws, permitting only altruistic surrogacy between close relatives and prohibiting commercial surrogacy. The regulatory evolution reflects the genuine complexity of the practice — a technology that can create families while also creating conditions for exploitation if not carefully managed.

For the Khans, the birth of Azad was a moment of private joy shared publicly. That public sharing, whatever its complications, opened a conversation the country needed to have.
