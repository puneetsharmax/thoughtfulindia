India's middle class—roughly 250-300 million people with annual incomes of 10-50 lakh rupees—consumes differently than previous generations. They possess options. Their parents chose among limited goods; they choose among abundance. This abundance creates distinct psychology: aspiration mediated by anxiety about maintaining status in an economy that remains uncertain.

The consumption patterns reveal the tension. New middle-class Indians buy Apple products and Samsung smartphones while browsing local market deals. They join premium gym memberships yet practice yoga at home. They eat at global franchises and street food. This is not incoherence but pragmatism: they desire the quality and status associated with premium products while maintaining awareness that income security is precarious and that financial discipline matters.

Housing represents the dominant anxiety. For previous generations, owning a house was symbol of arrival. For the new middle class, the house is often mortgaged over 20 years, consuming 40-50% of income. This creates persistent insecurity: job loss or income disruption threatens the mortgage. The housing obsession reflects this anxiety—real estate provides security that income no longer guarantees.

Education spending represents another anxiety manifesting as consumption. Middle-class parents invest substantially in children's education—tutoring, coaching, private schools—to secure their children's mobility. This isn't frivolous consumption but strategic investment in status maintenance. The coaching industry exists because parents believe that education is the primary mechanism through which their children can secure better positions. Without premium education, they fear downward mobility.

Consumer debt has expanded significantly. Credit cards, buy-now-pay-later services, and personal loans enable consumption that income alone wouldn't support. Yet this debt also creates anxiety: any income disruption threatens repayment. The psychological effect is precariousness mediated by consumption—spending makes life feel normal while underlying uncertainty persists.

There's also a generational divergence. Younger middle-class Indians (under 35) display more aspirational consumption, willing to take debt for experiences and status symbols. Older middle-class Indians (35-50) display more anxiety-driven saving and investment in children's education. Both groups share underlying insecurity: despite higher incomes than their parents, they feel less secure about maintaining status in a competitive economy.
