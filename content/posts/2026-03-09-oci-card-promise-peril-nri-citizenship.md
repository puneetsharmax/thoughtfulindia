The OCI card—created in 2005 to allow Indians with foreign citizenship to maintain Indian residence and business rights—reveals a fundamental tension in how India understands diaspora belonging. OCIs can own property, work, live indefinitely in India. Yet they cannot vote or hold political office. They're treated as Indian for economic purposes, foreign for political ones.

This inconsistency creates persistent anxiety. Restrictions on firearms, real estate purchases, professional licensing emerge irregularly. Each policy change sparks diaspora concerns about property rights. The underlying question: Are OCIs Indian or foreign?

The answer matters because millions maintain deep ties: property investment, family support, possible return. If India grants them such significant rights, shouldn't they have political voice? If India doesn't trust their judgment politically, why trust their economic participation?

Other democracies handle this differently. Israel and Mexico grant diaspora voting rights. India emphasizes diaspora contributions while explicitly excluding political participation.

The real issue is conceptual: India remains uncomfortable with diaspora nationalism—the idea that you can be Indian while being something else. The zero-sum approach (either full member or foreigner) doesn't fit globalized reality where people maintain multiple allegiances and partial memberships.

A more thoughtful system would recognize different diaspora categories: recent emigrants (stronger ties), ancient diaspora (attenuated identity), those contemplating return (need property security), those integrated elsewhere (need clarity on boundaries). Different statuses with different rights makes sense.

Until India resolves this conceptually, OCI remains a compromise satisfying few: diaspora lack political voice, India lacks clarity on diaspora role. The unresolved tension reflects India's discomfort with globalization itself.
