A study from Tel Aviv University produced a counterintuitive finding that generated significant popular attention: participants who ate a protein- and carbohydrate-rich breakfast that included a dessert item — chocolate, a cookie, cake — lost more weight and kept it off better over 32 weeks than participants who ate a low-carbohydrate, low-calorie breakfast without the sweet component.

The proposed mechanism was about hunger and cravings management. The conventional low-calorie dieting approach, which typically restricts sweets entirely, tends to intensify cravings for forbidden foods over time. Participants on restricted diets often hold out successfully for a period, then succumb to cravings in ways that derail their overall plan.

By incorporating a sweet into a nutrient-rich morning meal — when metabolism is most active and the body is best positioned to process calories and use glucose effectively — the dessert-at-breakfast approach aimed to preempt afternoon and evening cravings before they became overwhelming.

The substantial breakfast, with its protein and complex carbohydrate content, also maintained satiety more effectively than the low-calorie breakfast, reducing overall hunger throughout the day.

Nutritionists noted several caveats. This was one study with specific conditions, and the findings should not be taken as license to eat large amounts of sugar daily. The timing of caloric intake matters — a cookie at 7 a.m. is metabolically different from the same cookie at 10 p.m. And the protein component of the breakfast was likely doing substantial work.

The finding's practical takeaway: strict restriction often backfires. Planned indulgence, at the right time and within a nutrient-dense meal context, may be more sustainable than total denial.
