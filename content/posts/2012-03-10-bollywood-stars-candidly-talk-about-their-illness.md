A notable shift occurred in Bollywood's public culture when several prominent stars began speaking openly about health challenges that had previously been kept private — not out of a desire for sympathy, but as part of a broader cultural movement toward transparency about illness and the reduction of stigma around medical conditions.

Irrfan Khan's disclosure of his neuroendocrine tumor diagnosis in 2018 was among the most impactful. Khan, one of Hindi cinema's most respected actors, chose to write publicly about his diagnosis before the press could report it, framing his communication as a desire to control his own narrative and to model a certain kind of dignified engagement with uncertainty. His writing about the experience — honest, sometimes funny, sometimes frightening — generated widespread emotional response.

Sonali Bendre's simultaneous diagnosis with high-grade cancer, and her documentation of her treatment in the United States through social media, similarly opened public conversation about cancer's emotional and physical reality. She was frank about what treatment involved, about fear, about the support of family and friends, and about what the experience changed in her perspective.

Rishi Kapoor's extended treatment for leukemia in New York — which he also discussed candidly, through his characteristic bluntness — added another dimension: the experience of a major star confronting mortality and speaking about it without the protective distance of celebrity.

These disclosures had cumulative effects. They normalized serious illness as something that happens to admired, accomplished people. They reduced the shame that can prevent people from seeking diagnosis. And they contributed to a broader cultural conversation about illness, mortality, and what honest public communication about health can mean.
