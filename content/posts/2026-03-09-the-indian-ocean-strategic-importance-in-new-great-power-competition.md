The Indian Ocean is now where great power competition occurs. China controls Djibouti and has interests in Gwadar (Pakistan) and Hambantota (Sri Lanka). The US maintains Diego Garcia and partnerships throughout the region. India possesses geography—a 7,500 km coastline, proximity to critical chokepoints, and the naval capacity to project power. Yet it lacks presence sufficient to dominate the space. This tension defines contemporary Indian Ocean strategy.

Through 2026, roughly 40% of global maritime trade moved through the Indian Ocean, transiting through the Strait of Malacca, the Strait of Hormuz, and around the Cape. The region contains 35% of the world's maritime oil traffic. Control of these chokepoints—or strategic denial of that control—represents enormous economic leverage. A Chinese naval presence that threatens shipping creates risk premia for insurance and shipping costs. Indian naval capability that reassures trade partners becomes strategic asset.

China's Belt and Road Initiative explicitly targets Indian Ocean connectivity. Port investments in Sri Lanka, Bangladesh, and Myanmar create infrastructure that serves civilian commerce but can accommodate military assets. These ports become leverage points over local governments and potential naval staging areas. The US counter-strategy emphasizes "free and open Indo-Pacific"—maintaining shared maritime commons where no single power dominates. India increasingly aligns with this framing while maintaining autonomy.

![Naval ships at harbor]](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1558618666-fcd25c85cd64?auto=format&fit=crop&w=800&h=450&q=80)

India's strategic challenge is asymmetric. China's navy grows while India's remains constrained by budget and domestic priorities. China operates three aircraft carriers; India operates one and a second under construction. Chinese submarines outnumber Indian ones. Yet China faces its own constraints: long supply lines, limited naval bases outside home waters, and the need to defend its coastline. India can leverage geography if it invests in capability.

The strategic framework involves partnerships. The Quad (US, Japan, India, Australia) explicitly aims to balance Chinese power. India-US defense cooperation has deepened significantly. India's partnerships with Japan, Vietnam, and Indonesia create networks that collectively counter Chinese dominance. Yet India maintains hedging—it won't antagonize China unnecessarily and seeks commercial relationships alongside strategic rivalry.

By 2030, the question will be whether Indian naval capability matches Indian Ocean geography. If India continues underinvesting in maritime capability, the region defaults to Chinese influence by default. If India invests aggressively, it becomes a genuine strategic power balancer in the world's most economically significant ocean.
