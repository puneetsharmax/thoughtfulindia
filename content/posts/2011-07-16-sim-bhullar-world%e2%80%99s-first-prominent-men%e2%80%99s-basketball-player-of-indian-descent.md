At 7 feet 4 inches, Sim Bhullar is impossible to miss in any setting, but his height is the least remarkable thing about him. What makes Bhullar significant in the history of basketball is what he represents: the first player of South Asian Indian descent to reach the highest levels of the sport in North America.

Bhullar was born in Toronto to Punjabi parents who had immigrated from India, and grew up in a household where basketball was not the culturally obvious path. South Asian families in Canada and the United States have produced doctors, engineers, entrepreneurs, and academics in disproportionate numbers — but athletes who reach elite levels of major North American professional sports have been rare, and in basketball essentially nonexistent before Bhullar.

His trajectory through the college ranks at New Mexico State and into professional consideration drew immediate attention from the South Asian diaspora across North America and from basketball fans in India, where the sport has grown substantially in profile even as cricket retains overwhelming dominance. The symbolism of a turbaned Sikh player at this level resonated beyond sports — a visual argument about representation and possibility that communities sometimes need to see before they believe it.

The practical question of whether Bhullar's game translates to elite professional competition is a separate matter. At his size, every movement draws attention; the question is whether his skill set, still developing, can match what his dimensions promise.

Whatever his ultimate career, the significance of his visibility — the photo of a 7-foot-4 turbaned player in a major college gymnasium — is real and has already influenced how South Asian families think about sports as a domain their children can meaningfully inhabit.
