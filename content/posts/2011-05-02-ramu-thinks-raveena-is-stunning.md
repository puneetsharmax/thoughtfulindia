Ram Gopal Varma's public praise for Raveena Tandon—describing her as stunning and suggesting she remained one of Hindi cinema's most underrated presences—revived discussion of an actress whose career had followed an unusual trajectory: breakout stardom in the 1990s, a period of relative eclipse, and a later-career resurgence in roles that showcased a range her earlier commercial films had rarely demanded.

Tandon was one of the defining faces of 1990s Bollywood—memorable for the rain-soaked dance sequence in "Tip Tip Barsa Paani" from Mohra, for a series of commercially successful films with Akshay Kumar, and for a screen presence that communicated a particular combination of sensuality and self-possession unusual in an industry that often demanded one or the other but not both.

Her career took a different direction after her marriage in 2004. She stepped back from leading roles, raised her family, and returned to the industry on more selective terms. The roles she chose—including Ajay Devgn's Drishyam—demonstrated acting chops that her earlier mass entertainers had not always had occasion to deploy.

Varma's comment touched on something genuine about the Bollywood career cycle for women of Tandon's generation. Actresses who defined the 1990s were expected to fade with their youth, a career arc that the industry took as natural and that its leading women have spent the subsequent decades actively disrupting.

Tandon's continued visibility—as actress, producer, and social media presence with views on the industry that she delivers without diplomatic softening—represents a model of sustained relevance built on terms she has set herself.
