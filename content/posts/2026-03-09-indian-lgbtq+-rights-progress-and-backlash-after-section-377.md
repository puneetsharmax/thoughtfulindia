In September 2018, the Supreme Court of India partially decriminalized homosexuality, striking down the part of Section 377 that had criminalized consensual same-sex relationships. The judgment was celebrated as progressive. Yet nearly six years later, the gains remain precarious—legal rights without genuine social acceptance, and increasing political backlash against even incremental progress.

What happened in 2018 was significant: same-sex relationships were no longer criminal offenses. This meant LGBTQ+ Indians could theoretically exist openly without fear of prosecution. Yet the judgment deliberately left many questions unanswered. Marriage equality was not granted. Adoption rights for same-sex couples were not addressed. The trans-related provisions of Section 377 remained problematic. The judgment was calibrated precisely: do what's legally necessary to decriminalize, but don't mandate anything further.

In many countries, decriminalization would lead to rapid expansion of rights—marriage, adoption, healthcare, employment protections. In India, decriminalization led almost immediately to political backlash. Right-wing groups began mobilizing on the issue. Claims that "Hindu values" were under attack became mainstream political rhetoric. Some state governments passed laws contradicting the Supreme Court judgment. Public opinion shifted toward restriction rather than expanding acceptance.

What's visible in India is the tension between legal rights and social acceptance. You can have a Supreme Court judgment that says something is legal. That doesn't make it socially acceptable or enforce protection against discrimination. A same-sex couple can't be prosecuted for their relationship, but they can still be harassed, discriminated against in housing and employment, rejected by families, and face violence without recourse. Legal rights without social protection are incomplete.

The current situation reflects this precisely. Technically, LGBTQ+ Indians have more rights than they did before 2018. Practically, their lives have become more precarious as political organizations mobilize against them and social hostility becomes acceptable public discourse.

The challenge ahead is that further progress on marriage equality, adoption rights, and explicit anti-discrimination protections faces enormous political resistance. These aren't technical legal questions but fundamental questions about what Indian society will accept. The majority seems unwilling to move.

This creates a difficult position for LGBTQ+ activists: celebrate the decriminalization win while confronting the reality that legal rights remain limited and social conditions are worsening. Progress isn't linear in India; sometimes legal victories are followed by political backlash that erodes their meaning.

What's needed is not just legal change but cultural shift—creating space where LGBTQ+ existence is simply accepted as part of Indian society. That's a longer and slower process than court victories. It requires generational change, education, economic opportunity that reduces dependence on family networks that reject LGBTQ+ members. None of that is happening at sufficient scale in contemporary India.

The likely trajectory is stagnation: legal decriminalization persists as a baseline, but further expansion of rights faces intense political opposition. LGBTQ+ Indians gain incrementally in urban centers with greater anonymity and liberal constituencies, but remain unsafe in much of rural and smaller-town India. This slow expansion in spaces where acceptance exists, coupled with persistence of danger in less accepting spaces, is the pattern likely to continue.
