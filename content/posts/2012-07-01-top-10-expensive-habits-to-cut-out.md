Personal finance writing has a reliable genre that resurfaces whenever economic conditions make people anxious about their spending: the inventory of small daily expenditures that, aggregated over time, amount to significant sums. The implicit promise is that frugality in the details funds freedom in the aggregate.

The standard list tends to include the usual suspects: daily coffee shop purchases (the "latte factor," a concept popularized by David Bach), unused gym memberships, subscription services maintained through inertia rather than actual use, smoking at current cigarette prices, frequent restaurant meals versus home cooking, lottery tickets, premium cable packages watched for a fraction of their available content, impulse online purchases enabled by one-click buying, brand-name versus generic products across grocery categories, and the various small transaction fees — ATM fees, late payment fees, overdraft fees — that accumulate through minor financial disorganization.

The arithmetic behind these calculations is genuine. A $5 daily coffee is $1,825 a year; over a decade, invested at moderate returns, it becomes meaningful. A gym membership that costs $50 a month but is used twice represents very expensive individual workouts. Cigarettes at current prices represent a significant annual expenditure for a daily smoker.

The critique of this genre is also genuine. The "cut your lattes" framework puts the burden of financial insecurity on individual consumption choices while largely ignoring structural factors — stagnant wages, rising housing costs, healthcare expenses, student debt — that are not addressable through personal frugality at the margin. For people whose genuine financial problem is that they earn too little rather than spend too carelessly, the list of things to cut is both practically limited and potentially insulting.

Both things are true simultaneously: individual spending habits matter, and they are not the primary explanation for most financial distress.
