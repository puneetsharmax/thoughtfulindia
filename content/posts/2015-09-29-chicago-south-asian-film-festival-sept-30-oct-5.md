The Chicago South Asian Film Festival's fall edition offered audiences five days of cinema spanning the full range of contemporary filmmaking from India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal, and the global South Asian diaspora — a program that deliberately challenged the equation of South Asian film with commercial Bollywood productions.

Presented at venues in Chicago's cultural core, the festival program included recent award winners from international film circuits, documentaries addressing urgent social questions, and narrative features exploring the complexities of identity, migration, and belonging that animate so much of the best contemporary South Asian cinema.

South Asian filmmaking outside the Bollywood mainstream has produced some of the most formally adventurous and socially engaged cinema anywhere in the world over the past two decades. Directors like Anurag Kashyap, Ritesh Batra, and Deepa Mehta have built international reputations making films that engage honestly with social realities — caste discrimination, communal violence, the lives of women in patriarchal societies — that mainstream commercial cinema tends to elide or sanitize.

The diaspora dimension of the festival's programming reflects the particular experiences of South Asians navigating identity between cultures — a subject that has produced a rich body of film work from directors who grew up in the UK, Canada, the United States, and Australia while maintaining deep connections to their ancestral cultures.

Chicago's South Asian population, one of the largest in the United States with particular concentrations in the northern suburbs, provides both the audience and the community context that makes festivals like this viable. For many South Asian families in the Midwest, the annual film festival is a cultural touchstone — an opportunity to encounter their own stories told with seriousness and care.
