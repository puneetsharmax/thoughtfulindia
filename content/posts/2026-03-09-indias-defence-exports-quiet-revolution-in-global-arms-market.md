India's defence exports exceeded $2.5 billion in 2023—a threefold increase from 2019. Most products stay below public attention: missile systems, naval vessels, drones, artillery systems, ammunition. India now exports defence equipment to 70+ countries, competing in markets traditionally dominated by Russia, America, and Western Europe. This shift from being a defence importer to an exporter represents genuine strategic transition.

The trajectory is driven by several factors. India builds defence equipment for itself first—the world's largest standing military creates enormous internal demand. This generates production expertise, economies of scale, and feedback loops that improve quality. Israeli defence companies succeeded similarly: Israeli military requirement drove development; export markets followed.

Additionally, Indian defence products are cost-competitive. An Indian missile system costs 40-50% of American equivalents, with performance adequate for many requirements. For developing nations—African countries, Southeast Asian nations, Middle Eastern militaries—Indian systems offer capability at affordable prices. This positioning creates market.

There's also geopolitical positioning. India exports to countries where American or Russian arms come with political baggage. A Southeast Asian country buying from India avoids dependency on Cold War rivals. African nations appreciate alternatives to colonial-era relationships. Middle Eastern states value Indian neutrality. India's position as a non-aligned power—despite closer strategic alignment with America—provides genuine diplomatic advantage.

The export success also reflects manufacturing capability improving. Homegrown systems—Akash missiles, Brahmos cruise missiles, Tejas aircraft—were initially criticized but have matured into competitive products. Production has become more efficient. Quality control has improved. This is visible progress in India's defence-industrial complex.

However, there are limits. India remains dependent on foreign inputs for many defence products—engines from Russia, avionics from America, materials from Europe. Full technological independence remains distant. The export volumes, while growing, pale against Russian or American defence exports, which run into tens of billions. India's $2.5 billion is significant within India but modest globally.

Additionally, constraints exist: defence exports require government approval, political considerations influence purchasing decisions, and technological sophistication required for leading-edge systems remains concentrated in developed countries.

The trajectory is nonetheless real: India is becoming a genuine defence exporter, competing in markets globally, building export relationships that create diplomatic leverage. This doesn't make India a weapons superpower, but it does reflect the country's increasing industrial and strategic capability.
