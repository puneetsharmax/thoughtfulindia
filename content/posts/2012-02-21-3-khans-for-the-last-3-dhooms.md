Bollywood's Dhoom franchise has built itself into one of Hindi cinema's most reliable commercial properties through a consistent formula: slick production, elaborate action sequences, and the casting of a major star as the villain opposite the franchise's regular leads. The choice of villain has, in each installment, generated enormous anticipation and speculation.

The pattern of casting a "Khan" — one of Bollywood's three dominant male stars: Shah Rukh Khan, Aamir Khan, and Salman Khan — as the antagonist in successive Dhoom films became a subject of considerable industry and fan discussion. Each Khan brought his own screen persona and audience relationship to the villain role, and each was said to be transformative for the character.

The villains in the Dhoom films are not conventional antagonists but anti-heroes — charismatic thieves whose style and daring often overshadow the nominal heroes. This framing suits the Khan brand particularly well. Each of the three stars commands audience loyalty so strong that their characters, whatever their moral status within the narrative, become objects of identification rather than opposition.

The franchise's producers understood something that superhero films have since codified as industry wisdom: audiences often root for the character who is most alive on screen, regardless of which side of the law they occupy. Casting the most bankable stars as the antagonists guaranteed that the Dhoom films would produce the kind of villain fandom that generates lasting cultural conversation.

Whether the formula holds beyond three films — and which star might next step into the villain's shoes — remains a question the industry watches closely.
