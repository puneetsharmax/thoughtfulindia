On April 14, Ambedkar Jayanti, there's a curious split in how India remembers him. In government buildings and corporate offices, his portrait is garlanded. Speeches invoke his drafting of the Constitution. Ministers speak of his genius. Yet the caste system—the very phenomenon he devoted his life to attacking—remains the defining axis of social stratification in India. Ambedkar is revered and ignored simultaneously, which may be exactly the fate he feared.

B.R. Ambedkar's intellectual project was devastatingly simple: caste is not inevitable, not divinely ordained, not reformable from within. It must be destroyed. This was radical in 1920s India and remains radical today, because it rejects the gradualist fantasy that caste can be "improved" through education or inter-caste marriages. It insisted caste is a system of power requiring abolition, not rehabilitation.

The Constitution he drafted in 1949 reflected this conviction. Article 17 abolished untouchability. Articles 15-16 prohibited discrimination on caste grounds. The Directive Principles called for a society free from hierarchy. These were not modest proposals. They were a blueprint for unmaking centuries of social architecture.

Seventy-five years later, the scorecard is mixed. Legally, untouchability is indeed abolished. Reservations have created pathways for Scheduled Castes and Tribes into education and civil service. But the structural reality is unyielding: inter-caste marriage rates remain single digits. Urban high-castes still marry within their groups. Occupational segregation—the idea that certain professions are the preserve of certain castes—persists in agriculture and services. Violence against Dalits has not declined; sometimes it has grown more vicious.

What Ambedkar diagnosed was that caste isn't merely social preference—it's an entire knowledge system that teaches people their place and makes hierarchy feel natural. Affirmative action can crack this system at the margins but cannot demolish it. Only education, economic mobility, and cultural revolution can. India pursued the first somewhat, the second incompletely, and the third barely at all.

The paradox is that Ambedkar's intellectual influence has grown precisely as his political project has stalled. Every significant social movement in contemporary India—anti-caste activism, Dalit feminism, Ambedkarite Buddhism—invokes him. Yet mainstream discourse treats him as historical rather than prescriptive. We celebrate him without asking whether his central argument—that caste is incompatible with democracy—has been proven correct.

Look at recent data: Dalit students still face violence in universities. In rural India, Dalit and Muslim homes remain segregated in neighborhoods. Job markets show persistent caste discrimination in hiring. Women from lower castes experience intersectional violence—caste plus gender. These aren't failures of implementation. They're evidence that the constitutional promise was never fully accepted as legitimate by large portions of Indian society.

Where Ambedkar is most alive is in unexpected places. Dalit writers are producing powerful literature. Dalit entrepreneurs are building enterprises outside traditional caste-based professions. Inter-caste friendships and relationships are increasing, especially in cities. But this is despite the system, not because of Ambedkar's political vision being realized.

The question contemporary India should ask is whether Ambedkar was wrong about needing revolution or simply premature. Can a constitutional commitment to equality gradually erode caste, or was his diagnosis that only wholesale social reconstruction could work the accurate one? The evidence suggests he was right. Caste doesn't yield to incremental pressure. It adapts, it modernizes, it finds new languages—but it persists.

This is the uncomfortable truth on Ambedkar Jayanti: we've preserved the Constitution he imagined without building the society he described. Until we do, his legacy remains incomplete—celebrated, but not yet realized.
