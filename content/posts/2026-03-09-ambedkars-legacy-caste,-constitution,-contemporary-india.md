Every April 14, India observes Ambedkar Jayanti with a curious and revealing split. In government buildings and corporate offices, portraits of B.R. Ambedkar are garlanded. Ministers give speeches. The Constitution he drafted is celebrated. Yet simultaneously, the caste system—the very phenomenon he devoted his entire intellectual and political life to attacking—remains the defining axis of social stratification in contemporary India. Ambedkar is revered ceremonially while his central arguments are ignored systematically. He is lionized and contradicted simultaneously, which may be precisely the fate he feared and warned about.

B.R. Ambedkar's intellectual project was devastatingly clear and uncompromising. Caste is not inevitable. It is not divinely ordained or religiously necessary. It is not reformable from within through gradual education or inter-caste marriage. Caste is a system of oppression and hierarchy that must be abolished completely. This was radical in the 1920s and 1930s when he first articulated it, and remains radical today, because it rejects the gradualist fantasy—so appealing to those comfortable with existing hierarchies—that caste can be "improved" or "reformed" through incremental change. Ambedkar insisted that caste is a system of power requiring complete abolition, not rehabilitation.

The Constitution he drafted in 1949-1950 reflected this radical vision. Article 17 abolished untouchability completely. Articles 15 and 16 prohibited discrimination on caste grounds in education, employment, and public services. The Preamble's promise of equality before the law and equal protection was directed specifically against caste-based hierarchy. The Directive Principles envisioned a society free from such oppression. These were not modest proposals. They were an explicit blueprint for unmaking centuries of social architecture and consciousness.

Seventy-five years later, the scorecard is deeply mixed, revealing a constitutional victory coupled with sociological defeat. Legally, untouchability is indeed abolished—it's a crime to practice it explicitly. Reservations have created pathways for Scheduled Castes and Tribes into education and civil service that wouldn't exist otherwise. Some mobility has occurred. But the structural reality remains unyielding in most contexts: inter-caste marriage rates remain stubbornly single digits. Urban high-caste Indians still marry within their groups. Occupational segregation—the inherited idea that certain professions are properly the preserve of certain castes—persists in agriculture and many services. Violence against Dalits has not declined; periodic data suggest it has grown more vicious. Caste discrimination in housing, employment, and marriage remains systemic despite legal prohibition.

The problem Ambedkar diagnosed with precision was that caste isn't merely individual preference or cultural preference—it's an entire knowledge system, a cosmology that teaches people their proper place in a hierarchy and makes inequality feel natural, deserved, divinely ordained. Affirmative action can crack this system at the margins—a Dalit can become a doctor or civil servant despite traditional restrictions. But cracks cannot demolish the structure itself. Only comprehensive education, dramatic economic mobility, and cultural revolution—systematic reconstruction of values and consciousness—can do that. India pursued affirmative action somewhat successfully. It pursued economic development incompletely. It barely attempted cultural revolution against caste consciousness.

The paradox of contemporary India is that Ambedkar's intellectual influence has grown precisely as his political project has stalled. Every significant social movement—anti-caste activism, Dalit feminism, caste-critical scholarship—invokes his thought. Yet mainstream discourse treats him as historical rather than prescriptive. We celebrate him without asking whether his central argument—that caste is fundamentally incompatible with democracy and equality—has been proven correct by events.

Consider recent evidence: Dalit students still face violence and discrimination in universities. In rural India, Dalit and Muslim homes remain spatially segregated in neighborhoods. Employment discrimination by caste persists and is documented across studies despite legal prohibition. Women from lower castes experience intersectional violence—caste oppression plus gender oppression simultaneously. These aren't failures of implementation or gaps in legal frameworks. They're evidence that the constitutional promise of equality was never fully accepted as legitimate by large portions of Indian society.

Where Ambedkar remains most alive is in unexpected places outside formal politics. Dalit writers are producing powerful literature that names caste explicitly. Dalit entrepreneurs are building enterprises and accumulating capital outside traditional caste-determined professions. Inter-caste friendships and relationships are increasing, particularly in cities where anonymity permits deviation from family expectations. Social media is enabling collective action against caste discrimination. But this progress occurs despite systemic resistance, not because the Constitution has reformed consciousness.

The question contemporary India must ask is whether Ambedkar was wrong about requiring revolution or simply premature in his predictions. Can constitutional commitments to equality gradually erode caste consciousness through generational change? Or was his diagnosis that only wholesale social and cultural reconstruction could work the accurate one? The evidence suggests he was right. Caste does not yield to incremental legal pressure. It adapts, it modernizes, it finds new languages and rationalizations—but it persists as an organizing principle across generations.

Caste has transformed in form but not substance. Upper castes no longer explain hierarchy through explicit religious language (though some do). They explain it through "merit," "capability," "culture," "tradition"—all euphemisms that achieve the same effect: justifying why higher castes should occupy higher positions. Caste has become modern, professionalized, digitized. But it remains.

This is the uncomfortable truth that Ambedkar Jayanti celebrations often avoid: we've preserved the Constitution he imagined without building the society he described. We've adopted his words without embracing his vision. We celebrate the constitutional architect while rejecting the social revolution he insisted was necessary. Until we do that revolutionary work—the genuine reconstruction of consciousness, relationships, and institutions—Ambedkar's legacy remains incomplete. The words are enshrined. The work is unfinished. And the caste system, against which he dedicated everything, persists.
