Some people seem to absorb new skills and information with remarkable speed while others struggle to make the same progress despite equal time and effort. The reasons are more varied — and more actionable — than most people assume.

Mindset is a significant factor, and Carol Dweck's research on growth versus fixed mindsets has become foundational in understanding it. People who believe their abilities can be developed through effort and strategy tend to outperform those who believe intelligence and talent are fixed. The growth mindset learner treats failure as information; the fixed mindset learner treats it as verdict.

Prior knowledge also matters enormously. Learning is not an additive process — it's connective. New information sticks better when the brain has an existing framework to attach it to. This is why experts learn new developments in their field faster than novices: they're building on a dense structure of prior understanding. The practical implication is that building a solid foundation, even if it seems slow, accelerates everything that follows.

The method of practice matters as much as the quantity. Deliberate practice — characterized by focused attention, immediate feedback, and pushing just beyond the current skill level — produces far greater gains than unfocused repetition. Simply doing something more doesn't make you better; doing it with intention and close attention does.

Sleep is underappreciated as a learning tool. Memory consolidation — the process by which new information moves from short-term to long-term memory — happens primarily during sleep, particularly during certain stages. Pulling all-nighters to prepare for a test is almost literally counterproductive.

Finally, fast learners tend to be highly metacognitive — they think about how they're thinking. They notice when a method isn't working and switch approaches. They test themselves rather than just reviewing. They're learning how to learn, not just learning.
